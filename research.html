Invariant Representations

Often we want a representation of our data that is invariant to known outside factors: we want scans invariant to site/scanner (data harmonization), image representations invariant to scale and/or rotations (e.g. SIFT), and predictions that are uninformative of protected classes ("fair" predictions).

As it turns out these are all cases of a general representation learning problem, where we'd like to learn code z that represents data x well, yet be uninformed of an outside variable s: \[\min L(z|x) \text{ subject to }z \perp s\] Here, L is a general loss function which could be an auto-encoder loss, some prediction task, or something entirely different.

This series of papers starts with our NeurIPS paper (NeurIPS 2018), which details how to build such a representation using a Variational Auto-Encoder (VAE), and a relaxation of the independence constraint to mutual information. The intution of the result is that compression and conditional reconstruction leads to invariance.

Forthcoming work places this in the context of data harmonization for diffusion MRI.

  1. Invariant Representations without Adversarial Training
    D Moyer, S Gao, R Brekelmans, G Ver Steeg, A Galstyan, NIPS 2018
  2. Scanner Invariant Representations for Diffusion MRI Harmonization
    D Moyer, G Ver Steeg, CMW Tax, PM Thompsom, Preprint


Continuous Connectivity

Connectomics (brain connectivity) is usually synonymous with graph theory. Brain regions or structures form nodes and some evidence of connectivity forms the edges (structural connections, functional correlations, etc.).

Unfortunately, in humans there isn't agreement on which atlas of regions is correct; even conditioning on the subject's age, sex, the spatial scale, etc., it's not clear that the "one true atlas" even exists.

Are intuitions of brain connectivity wholly reliant upon the concept of brain atlases (parcellation)? In our first paper (MICCAI 2016/MedIA 2017), we answered this in the negative: we demonstrated a parcellation free model of connectivity, one that removes regions in favor of the continuum of the original spatial domain, the cortical surface.

(As a technicality, this doesn't have to be the brain at all, but any spatial network. We haven't applied these methods to other spatial networks...yet ;D )

After creating the initial model, we explored an extension that learned parcellations from a continuous density (IPMI 2017, MICCAI-MLMI 2018), and one that directly analyzed densities in a regression setting (forthcoming).

  1. Continuous Representations of Brain Connectivity using Spatial Point Processes
    D Moyer, B Gutman, N Jahanshad, J Faskowitz, PM Thompson, MedIA 2017
  2. A Restaurant Process Mixture Model for Connectivity Based Parcellation of the Cortex
    D Moyer, B Gutman, N Jahanshad, PM Thompson, IPMI 2017
